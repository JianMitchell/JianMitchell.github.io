Overview

The Agile methodology emerged within the early 2000s, which was created due to the limitations of the SDLC approaches at that given time period. Therefore, The Agile Manifesto was signed in collaboration with seventeen software developers in 2001, emphasizing and establishing core values such as adaptability, collaboration, and customer satisfaction over rigid planning and documentation.

Compared to prior approaches that emphasized the implementation of an entire projects plan within its first phase, the Agile model breaks the development into iterations called sprints. Basically, these sprints last between-two-to-four weeks through software increments, which help development teams gather feedback early to readjusted their approach in the usage of the software and its changing requirements.

The Agile methodology prioritizes face-to-face communication, continuous shareholder engagement, and rapid response to change. Therefore, needing daily meetings, sprint planning sessions, and retrospectives to constantly align teams towards maintaining flexibility. This approach has become the leading methodology in modern software development for web applications, mobile apps, and products that require frequent updates.

Agile Frameworks and Practices

Scrum

This is one of the most popular Agile frameworks, as it provides defined roles for development teams, sprints, and ceremonies that would regularly allow for daily communication, sprint planning, reviews, and retrospectives. In the end, Scrum provides developers with a well-founded structure, while maintaining flexibility.

Kanban

This framework is for a visual workflow management system that continuously delivers without fixed iterations. Basically, a project manager used to establish milestones through work-in-progress limits that prevent bottlenecks in production. In the end, Kanban is meant for maximum flexibility for development teams handling various workloads.

Extreme Programming (XP)

This framework is focused on technical practices derived from test development, pair programming, continuous integration, and refactoring. Basically, the very idea behind this one is to be not only fluent in development, but excel through disciplined engineering practices.

Key Agile Ceremonies

Ceremony Duration Purpose
Daily Communication 15 minutes Synchronized team activities
Sprint Planning 2-4 hours Defined sprint goals
Sprint Review 1-2 hours Completed work shared with shareholders
Sprint Retrospective 1-2 hours Reflection on the process and identify improvements
Backlog Refinement 1 hour Clarification on upcoming work items and estimates

Agile Best Practices

  • User Stories: This practice is meant to provide developers a new perspective. Basically, users of their system can voice requirements based on their personal wants and how it would benefit the development of the system in the long run.
  • Definition of Done: This practice is reminiscent of project management systems, as it dictates clear criteria before the work is considered complete, thus ensuring quality standards and requirements.
  • Continuous Integration: This practice helps developers integrate code into repositories like GitHub, which helps automate builds and creates a testing environment for issues early on.
  • Velocity Tracking: This practice is used to measure the teams output through sprints, which is essentially a record of improvement and prediction on future accuracy and capacity.
  • Burndown Charts: This practice is used to provide a visual representation on what work is completed against the amount of time it took to accomplish. Basically, this is mostly used to stay on track during sprints.
  • Product Backlog: This practice prioritizes a list of features, enhancements, and fixes. Basically, a guide that showcases the progression through written files or time stamps on the development of the software.
Example: Agile Transformation

Companies can develop Agile scaling models with autonomous groups organized through guilds. Basically, a squad of developers can operate like a mini-startup with end-to-end responsibility for specific features, which means that each organized group under a certain division/guild, can work on certain structures that can eventually be pieced together like a puzzle for the final system. Therefore, the Agile scaling models emphasize autonomy, alignment, and continuous improvement, providing flexibility and constant improvement.

When to Use Agile

Ideal For

  • Constantly changing requirements
  • Collaboration with customers
  • A Rapidly changing market
  • Innovative products
  • Experienced teams

Challenging For

  • Fixed contracts
  • Distributed teams
  • Regulatory compliance
  • Inexperienced teams
  • Large enterprise systems
